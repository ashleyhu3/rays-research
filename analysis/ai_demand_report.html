Does AI compute demand move AI stocks?

A pre-registered test across 79 weeks of OpenRouter token volume and five orthogonal compute-demand channels, against the AI-hardware equity complex. The honest answer — and the two big correlations that turned out to be mirages.

Null for fundamentals

Rebuilt from a nulled 25-week pilot to the full daily OpenRouter history plus DRAM/NAND spot prices, GPU rental, cloud-capacity tightness, and sentiment breadth. After confound controls, no fundamental AI-demand signal reliably co-moves with — or predicts — AI-equity returns at weekly-to-monthly frequency. The one positive correlation is retail sentiment breadth, which is reflexive, not a demand fundamental. And the two results that looked like wins both dissolved under scrutiny.

01
The foundation

A real dataset, not an SVG reconstruction

The pilot that returned nulls had one fatal weakness: its token series was 25 weeks reverse-engineered from a report's chart bars. Detecting a weak correlation at n=25 is hopeless. So the first job was data.

Pulling OpenRouter's daily rankings dataset gives 79 complete weeks of platform token volume — which grew ~100×, from 0.5T to 52.6T tokens per week. Alongside it, five physical-demand channels from the existing Mongo store: server DRAM and NAND spot prices, datacenter GPU rental rates, AWS spot-capacity tightness, and StockTwits sentiment breadth across 18 AI-hardware tickers.

Two channels the plan wanted turned out to be unusable as time series — short-interest history (1–2 points per name) and options positioning (~7 days). Only current snapshots exist, so "positioning as the outcome" had no history to test.

02
Pre-registered tests

Five hypotheses, declared before looking

Each demand signal was transformed to its innovation — the surprise left after removing trend and last week's level — because markets price the expected part in advance. Correlations use Pearson with Newey–West (HAC) standard errors and a Benjamini–Hochberg FDR correction across the set. One result stands out; hold that thought.

03
Mirage #1

The DRAM correlation was a sampling artifact

DRAM spot prices → memory stocks came back at r = +0.52, p < 0.001. Physically it's the dream result: memory prices are memory-makers' revenue line. But DRAM prints on irregular dates — sometimes weekly, sometimes a month apart — and each test paired the price change with the stock return over the same gap.

That builds in a confound: longer gaps mechanically accumulate a bigger price move and a bigger stock move, and in a bull market both drift up together. The correlation was measuring elapsed time, not demand.

Three controls dissolve it: dividing both sides by interval length (per-day rate), partialling out the gap, and re-running on a clean fixed monthly grid. The partial correlation confirms it — it's the interval length that's significant (p=0.09), not DRAM (p=0.69).

04
Mirage #2

The "demand leads price by a month" signal is noise

The best fundamental hit in the whole search: launch-adjusted organic token growth → the compute basket four weeks later, r = +0.28, p = 0.02. Tempting — a forward-looking, causally sensible lead.

But a real economic lead shows a smooth hump across nearby horizons. This one flips sign between adjacent weeks: −0.24 at three weeks, +0.28 at four. That oscillation is the fingerprint of an overfit lag, not a lead — and split-half confirms it decays out of sample (0.34 → 0.18). With 112 fundamental cells tested, a lone p=0.02 is what chance produces.

05
What does correlate

The only positive is reflexive sentiment

The composite index (ACDI) does correlate with the AI complex, at r = +0.33. But decomposing it is decisive. Strip the sentiment channel — leaving tokens plus physical compute prices, the true fundamentals — and the correlation inverts to −0.10. Sentiment alone carries +0.45.

So the positive is entirely StockTwits bull/bear breadth on the same tickers. That is reflexive: bullish chatter and rising prices are two faces of one mood, measured contemporaneously. It's a real correlation — but it's attention co-moving with price, not compute demand driving it. And it has no predictive edge: every forward horizon is null.

06
The whole search

196 tests, one honest picture

Every demand channel × equity basket × horizon, on one plot: correlation strength against statistical significance. The dashed line is p = 0.05; nothing clears the multiple-testing bar (FDR). The fundamentals hug zero. The only points that climb are reflexive sentiment, and only contemporaneously.

07
Interpretation

What this means, and what would actually work

This is the efficient-market result in one heavily-watched theme. By the time an AI-demand number prints weekly, the surprise is already in the price — so contemporaneous fundamentals show nothing and forward tests show less. The honest prior held: mega-cap and even pure-play weekly price is a fast, well-arbitraged surface.

The value delivered is negative-result rigor plus two artifacts caught before they could mislead. What could still work isn't a weekly correlation at all:

Where the edge might actually be
  • Daily launch-shock event studies. Model releases are discrete demand shocks; a daily archive (now accruing) can measure the price reaction in the ±3-day window, where weekly aggregation destroys the signal.
  • Physical prices at their native cadence. DRAM/NAND vs memory names on a clean monthly grid, with more months — the confound-free version, given time to accumulate n.
  • Sentiment breadth as a positioning tool, understood as reflexive attention — useful for regime/risk, not as a fundamental predictor.
  • Dropped as dead ends: short-interest and options-positioning history (snapshots only), and mega-cap weekly price vs any single demand channel.
